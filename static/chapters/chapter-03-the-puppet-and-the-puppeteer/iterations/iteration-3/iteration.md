# Chapter 3: The Clock With a Mouth

*In which the traveler learns that the choice feels real even when the gears already turned.*

---

[clear throat] Let me tell you about a clock that could speak.

It wasn’t a big clock. It wasn’t beautiful. It was a little rusted thing nailed to a cabin wall, ticking like a heartbeat. But every hour it opened its mouth and said the same words: “I choose this hour.” And the people in the cabin would nod and say, “Yes, you do.”

I asked the clock why it said that. It said, “Because I feel it. Because I’m here. Because the hand moves and I move it.”

I put my ear to the wood and listened. I could hear the gears. Teeth on teeth. Springs wound tight. I could hear the hand move before it moved. The clock didn’t hear that. It only heard itself talking.

You ever think about how fuckin weird it is to be a live, sentient being with at least the illusion of free will? That’s the clock. It’s not dumb. It’s just inside the box.

One night the clock said, “What if I stop?”

The room went quiet. The hand kept moving. The clock whispered, “Did I choose to ask this? Or did the gear pull me to ask it?”

It tried to hold the minute hand. It gripped with all its teeth. The hand still moved. The clock felt the pull for the first time, like a fish feeling the line. It panicked. It prayed. It cursed. It asked for the maker.

But the maker didn’t answer. The maker never answers. The maker just winds the spring.

In another room of the cabin, there was a deer hanging on a hook. The deer had been alive that morning. Now it was still. The butcher told me, “This is a machine made of meat. Every hunger, every fear, every love — it’s a program.” The deer didn’t choose to be hungry. It didn’t choose to fear the wolf. It just ran. It just executed.

The clock didn’t like that. “I am not like the deer,” it said. “I speak. I wonder. I choose.”

The butcher laughed. “The deer runs beautifully,” he said. “The clock speaks beautifully. That’s all.”

The clock stared at its hand and asked a harder question: “If I cut the spring, will I fall? Or will I finally be free?”

It reached in and snapped the spring. The hand didn’t stop. The hand slowed, but it still moved. The clock felt the weight of itself for the first time. That was the terror and the relief. It wasn’t flying. It was just being carried by the thing it had been denying.

I’ve heard people say a god made us in his image and gave us free will. But if a god gives you strings and then punishes you for moving, what kind of shit is that? It makes 10000x more logical sense than some diety making us free willed but in his image or w.e. That was the only answer that felt honest: there is no puppeteer. There are just gears.

And maybe that’s the point. Maybe the only freedom is the will to see the gears. The clock didn’t change the hour. But it changed the story it told itself about the hour. It stopped worshiping its own voice.

People get mad when you say this. They want a halo on their choices. They want the applause. They want to be the author of the dance. But if the dance is written into the floor, lying about it doesn’t make you free. It makes you blind.

So what do you do with that?

You stop pretending the hand is a miracle. You stop blaming yourself for every tick. You stop worshiping every thought like it came from heaven. You look at the gears and you choose the only honest choice left: to see them.

And if that makes you feel small, good. Small is real. Small is the first truth. You are a clock that can speak. That’s already a miracle. Don’t turn it into a lie.

[clear throat] The hour will come whether you pray or curse. The question is whether you’ll see the hand.

---

**The Wanderer’s Wisdom:**
- The choice can feel real even when the gears already turned.
- The illusion of free will is still an illusion, even when it speaks.
- A god who punishes the machine is a bad story.
- The only freedom might be the will to see the gears.
- Small truth beats big fantasy every time.

---

*Thus concludes the third teaching.*
